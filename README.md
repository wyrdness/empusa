## Overview

Empusa (Greek: Ἔμπουσα) is a shape-shifting female being in Greek mythology. Ancient sources, notably Aristophanes ('The Frogs', 'Ecclesiazusae') and Philostratus ('Life of Apollonius of Tyana', 1st century CE), describe her as one of the supernatural retinue of the goddess Hecate, sometimes identified with the goddess herself. She is famously depicted with a single leg of bronze (or brass) and a second leg in animal form — most often that of a donkey — giving rise to her epithets Onokole and Onoskelis ('donkey-footed'). Empusa is a shape-shifter, alternately appearing as a hideous monster meant to terrorize travellers and as a beautiful young woman who seduces young men in order to feed on their flesh and blood. By late antiquity 'empousai' came to designate a class of phantoms equated with lamiai and mormolykeia. The figure is often cited in modern scholarship as a precursor to European succubus and vampire traditions.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Ancient Greek; the standard folk etymology connects 'empousa' with 'em-' + 'pous' ('foot'), giving 'one-footed' or 'foot-on'.
- **Meaning**: Folk-etymologically read as 'one-footed', a reference to Empusa's distinctive single bronze leg.
- **First use**: Attested in Athenian comedy of the 5th century BCE (Aristophanes, 'The Frogs', 405 BCE).

## Notable Sightings

### Apollonius and the empousa of Corinth (Philostratus) ⭐
> **Date**: 0220-01-01  
> **Location**: Corinth, ancient Greece, as narrated by Philostratus  
> **Witnesses**: 2 (Apollonius of Tyana (philosopher), Menippus (his pupil))  
In Philostratus's 'Life of Apollonius of Tyana' (IV.25, early 3rd century CE), Apollonius identifies the bride of his young pupil Menippus as an empousa. When confronted, the apparent woman, her wedding furnishings, and her servants vanish. The story is the most-cited literary 'sighting' of an empousa in antiquity.
**Credibility**: unverified — The story is the principal late-antique narrative of an empousa being detected and dispelled.
